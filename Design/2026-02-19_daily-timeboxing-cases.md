## Case: Planning the workday

Tommi wants to start his workday knowing what he'll work on and roughly when, so he can focus without making prioritization decisions mid-day. He has tasks carried over from yesterday plus new ones in his head, but he doesn't know how they'll all fit into the hours available.

**Status data**
- It's 8:45 on a Tuesday morning
- Tommi has 5 tasks on his list: 3 carried over from yesterday (incomplete), 2 he thought of this morning
- He knows roughly which feel most important, but hasn't estimated how long the new ones will take
- The two new tasks: "write architecture overview" (vague scope) and "review PR from teammate" (unknown size)
- The 3 carried-over tasks: "send client proposal draft" (urgent), "update onboarding docs" (medium), "tidy project notes" (low)
- Working day is 9:00–17:00; he plans to take a 30-minute lunch break around noon
- He does NOT know how many of these tasks will realistically fit in one day
- He has a vague memory of a meeting this afternoon but hasn't confirmed the time

**Variations**
- Tommi is working on a client project with externally set deadlines — priorities are imposed from outside and the challenge is fitting mandatory tasks around a hard constraint
- It's Monday after a long weekend and the backlog is unusually large (9 tasks, a mix of personal projects and work obligations)

## Case: Rescheduling an overrun task

Tommi is mid-task when he realizes it will take significantly longer than planned. He needs to update his day plan so the rest of the day stays realistic, but doing so requires his attention right when he's deepest in the work.

**Status data**
- It's 11:20 on a Wednesday
- Tommi has been working on "write architecture doc" since 10:30 — he planned it as a 1-hour slot
- Meaningful progress made, but the task clearly needs at least another 90 minutes
- His current plan for the rest of the day: lunch 12:00–12:30, "code review" 12:30–13:30, "write tests" 13:30–15:30, "admin tasks" 15:30–16:30
- He has NOT committed externally to which tasks will be done today
- He does NOT know whether to push lunch later, shorten a following task, or drop one task entirely
- "write tests" has subtasks: 3 units started, 4 still untouched — he has no sense of which he could defer

**Variations**
- The overrun is small (15 minutes) rather than large — Tommi must decide whether rearranging is worth the interruption to his focus at all
- The task has subtasks partially done; he wants to carry over only the unfinished subtasks, not the whole task

## Case: Accounting for untracked time

Tommi's day has been interrupted by short breaks and unplanned helping — each individually reasonable, but none recorded. By mid-afternoon his schedule no longer matches reality, and he can't tell whether he's behind because of the interruptions or because his estimates were wrong.

**Status data**
- It's 15:30; Tommi's working day ends at 17:00
- He has taken two unlogged breaks (~10 min and ~20 min) and spent roughly 40 minutes helping a colleague debug an issue
- His schedule still shows "write tests" as in progress — it was started at 13:30 but has barely advanced
- He has 2 tasks remaining that originally together estimated ~2 hours
- Tommi does NOT know how much of today was active focused work vs. interruptions
- He suspects his estimates were off, but can't tell by how much or why

**Variations**
- The unplanned time was a single long conversation (90 min) that Tommi considers genuinely important and wants to log as meaningful work — not as lost time
- The interruptions were all short and felt unproductive — Tommi wants to flag them as distractions, not contributions

## Case: Planning around a calendar event

Tommi is mid-way through planning or executing his day when he realizes a meeting blocks a significant chunk of time he had already allocated to tasks. He needs to reorganize his plan around it.

**Status data**
- It's 9:15 and Tommi has just finished placing 5 tasks into his day plan (filling 9:00–17:00 with no gaps)
- He just noticed a calendar reminder for a 90-minute team meeting starting at 14:00 — he had forgotten it when planning
- His current plan has "refactor module X" (13:00–14:30) and "write release notes" (14:30–16:00) now in direct conflict with the meeting
- He doesn't know whether to move both tasks earlier, split them around the meeting, or drop one
- He has no other fixed commitments today besides this meeting

**Variations**
- Tommi discovers the conflict at 13:50 — 10 minutes before the meeting starts — instead of at the beginning of the day
- There are two calendar events in the afternoon that together take 2.5 hours, neither of which was factored into the morning plan

## Case: Reviewing the day at wrap-up

At the end of the day Tommi wants to understand what he actually accomplished, feel settled about it, and carry useful insight into tomorrow. This is hardest on days when abstract or long-running work didn't produce visible results — those days feel wasted even when they weren't.

**Status data**
- It's 17:00 on a Thursday
- Tommi completed 3 of the 5 planned tasks; 2 were not started
- He spent most of the day on "draft architecture document" — it's roughly 70% done, not marked complete
- He took unlogged breaks and spent ~40 minutes helping a colleague (neither recorded)
- He does NOT know the actual time spent on any individual task
- He does NOT know whether the two unstarted tasks were dropped due to time pressure, procrastination, or a legitimate decision to reprioritize

**Variations**
- It was a productive day — all planned tasks completed — and Tommi wants to note what made it work well so he can replicate it
- The most important task ("submit proposal") was not completed; Tommi needs to decide whether to reschedule it or escalate its priority for tomorrow
